Symmetric matrix and positive-definite matrix

2021-12-30

This is the notes of Lecture 25 from MIT linear algebra course. The content will help my future research work.

1. Symmetric matrix

1.1. Definition:

If a real matrix $A$ satisfies the following property:
$$
A=A^T,
$$
then $A$ is a symmetric matrix.

1.2. Properties:

  1. The eigenvalues of a symmetric matrix are real.
  2. The eigenvectors are orthogonal (or we should say the eigenvectors can be chosen to be orthogonal/orthonormal).

why eigenvalues are real? Prove it! (hint: $\bar xx$ is the length of $x$ and is positive)

1.3. Comparison with usual matrices:

  1. Usual matrix:
    $$
    A=S\Lambda S^{-1},
    $$
           where $S$ is the matrix of eigenvectors and $\Lambda$ is a diagonal matrix of eigenvalues.

  2. Symmetric matrix:
    $$
    A= Q\Lambda Q^{-1}=Q\Lambda Q^T,
    $$
           where $Q$ is an orthonomal matrix so $Q^{-1}=Q^T$.

1.4. Extend symmetric matrix to complex matrix:

If a good matrix is defined to be a matrix whose eigenvalues are all real and eigenvectors are orthogonal, then the matrix is symmetric if it is real, or the matrix is Hessian ($A=\overline{A^T}={\bar A}^T$) if it is complex.

1.5. Spectral theorem for symmetric matrix:

$$
\begin{align*}
A &= Q\Lambda Q^{-1} \newline
&= \begin{bmatrix}
q_1 & q_2 & q_3 & \dots
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}
\lambda_{1} \newline
& \lambda_{2} \newline
& & \lambda_{3} \newline
& & & \ddots
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}
q_1^T \newline
q_2^T \newline
q_3^T \newline
\vdots
\end{bmatrix} \newline
&= \lambda_1q_1q_1^T+\lambda_2q_2q_2^T+\lambda_3q_3q_3^T+\dots
\end{align*}
$$
where $q_iq_i^T, i=1,2,\dots$ are projection matrices, since $q_i$’s are unit vectors and are pairwise.
So every symmetric matrix is a combination of perpendicular projection matrices.

2. Positive-definite matrix

Since the eigenvalues are real, it is natural to wonder whether the eigenvalues are positive or negative. This will introduce the problem of positive definite matrix.

2.1. Definition:

If a real symmetric matrix $A$ satisfies the following condition:
$$
\forall z \text{ is a nonzero real column vector, } z^TAz \text{ is positive.}
$$
Or if a Hessian matrix $A$ satisfies the following condition:
$$
\forall z \text{ is a nonzero complex column vector, } z^*Az \text{ is positive.}
$$
Then $A$ is a positive-definite matrix.

2.2. Properties:

  1. Positive-definite matrix is symmetric ($A=A^T$, for real matrix) or Hessian ($A=\overline{A^T}={\bar A}^T$, for complex matrix).

  2. All eigenvalues are positive.

  3. All sub-determinants are positive for positive-definite matrix.
    e.g. $
    \begin{bmatrix}
    5 & 3 \newline
    3 & 5
    \end{bmatrix}$ is a positive-definite matrix. Its sub-determinants, $
    det(\begin{bmatrix}
    5
    \end{bmatrix})$ and $
    det(\begin{bmatrix}
    5 & 3 \newline
    3 & 5
    \end{bmatrix})$ are both positive.

  4. All pivots are positive.

3. Appendix

  1. For symmetric matrix, the signs of the eigenvalues are the same as the signs of the pivots. Also, the number of positive pivots equals the number of positive eigenvalues.

  2. For general matrix, $product$ $of$ $the$ $pivots = determinant = product$ $of$ $eigenvalues$.
    2.1. The first equation is easy to prove (Gaussian elimination does not change determinant). The first equation applies to all square matrices.
    2.2. The second equation is that $det(A-\lambda I)=(\lambda_1-\lambda)(\lambda_2-\lambda)\dots$ is a function about $\lambda$. Take $\lambda=0$ then we will get $det(A)=\lambda_1\lambda_2\dots$. Second equation applies for all square matrices.
